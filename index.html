1. White Hat Visualization: G7 Emissions Index (2014–2023)

White Hat Write-up

This visualization utilizes the OECD Greenhouse Gas Emissions Inventories dataset, tracking total emissions (excluding Land Use) in kilograms of CO2-equivalent per person from 2014 to 2023. Per-capita data was selected for an equitable comparison of carbon intensity relative to population size, instead of total mass, which is often biased toward larger countries.

For effortless comprehension, the dataset was filtered to the G7 nations. This represents a transparent, non-arbitrary group of advanced economies, avoiding the confusion of including all 95 entities from the raw data. A line chart shows temporal trends, using a manually assigned high-contrast color palette to ensure geographic neighbors, like Canada and the United States, are instantly distinguishable. A horizontal reference line at the 100-index level provides a visual anchor to identify nations that successfully lowered emissions relative to the start of the decade.

To meet advanced transformation requirements, a Base-Year Indexing calculation was applied: (Current Year Value / 2014 Value) * 100. Setting 2014 as the "100" baseline focuses the visualization on the relative rate of change. This approach is more honest for general audiences; it prevents the volume of larger economies from visually overwhelming the significant reductions made by smaller nations, allowing a direct comparison of decarbonization progress. All titles and labels describe the visualization accurately, ensuring transparency.

The indexed trends reveal a clear divergence in G7 performance. The United Kingdom is the leader in per-capita reductions, with a consistent downward trajectory well below its baseline. This suggests effective decoupling of economic activity from emissions. In contrast, the United States and Italy show flatter trajectories. While making minor gains, their lines remain near the 100-index anchor, indicating slower decarbonization. This index demonstrates that while the UK substantially shrunk its footprint, progress in the US and Italy has remained relatively stagnant by comparison.

It also provided technical guidance on the Tableau indexing formula and helped refine the write-up's structure to ensure it addressed all rubric criteria, such as data transformation and ethical design transparency.
